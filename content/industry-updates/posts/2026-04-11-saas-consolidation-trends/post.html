Industry Update: SaaS Consolidation and What It Means for Your Stack

Remember when the solution to every business problem was "there's an app for that"?

For the past decade, companies accumulated software tools like collectibles—each department adding their preferred solutions, integration be damned.

The average mid-sized company now uses over 200 SaaS applications. That's not efficiency; that's digital hoarding. And businesses are finally waking up to the consequences: spiraling costs, security vulnerabilities, integration nightmares, and teams spending more time managing tools than using them.

Why Consolidation Is Accelerating

Factor 1: Economic Pressure

The era of cheap capital is over. CFOs face increasing pressure to demonstrate ROI on every expense. SaaS budgets that once grew 30% annually are now under scrutiny.

Factor 2: Integration Fatigue

Teams are exhausted by context-switching between tools that don't communicate. The productivity gains promised by specialized tools are negated by the friction of moving between them.

Factor 3: Platform Maturity

Major platforms have expanded to cover functions that once required specialized tools. The gap between all-in-one platforms and best-of-breed solutions has narrowed significantly.

Factor 4: Security and Compliance

More tools mean more attack surfaces, more vendor relationships to manage, and more compliance complexity. Consolidation reduces risk exposure.

How to Evaluate Your Stack

The Stack Audit Process

Step 1: Complete Inventory - Document every SaaS tool: name, users, cost, renewal dates, integrations, usage metrics

Step 2: Overlap Analysis - Identify tools with overlapping functionality

Step 3: Usage Assessment - Determine active users vs. seats, feature utilization, team satisfaction

Step 4: Strategic Alignment - Evaluate against critical business functions, integration value, scalability, vendor stability

Key Takeaways

  • The era of unconstrained SaaS growth is ending: Economic pressure, integration fatigue, and platform maturity are driving consolidation
  • Audit before you act: Understanding your current stack is essential before making changes
  • Platform-first approach: Building around core platforms reduces complexity and improves integration
  • Protect critical specialized tools: Not everything should be consolidated—keep what provides unique value
  • Manage change carefully: Technology migration success depends on people, not just tools

Action Steps

  1. Conduct a complete SaaS inventory this week—every tool, every user, every cost
  2. Identify your top 5 consolidation candidates based on overlap and underutilization
  3. Evaluate your core platform options for future stack strategy
  4. Calculate your true SaaS costs including overhead, not just subscriptions
