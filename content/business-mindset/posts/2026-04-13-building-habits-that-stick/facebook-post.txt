=== PART 1: Hook & Problem (No links) ===

You've made the commitment dozens of times.

Exercise daily. Read more books. Wake up earlier. Practice that skill.

For a week or two, you follow through.

Then life happens. Motivation fades. The habit quietly dies.

Here's the statistic that should wake you up:

80% of New Year's resolutions fail by February.

And it's NOT about willpower.

Most people who fail at habits are disciplined in other areas of their lives.

The problem isn't your character.

The problem is your APPROACH.

You're trying to build habits using motivation—which is a FEELING that fluctuates.

You're making habits too big for your brain to accept.

You're fighting your environment instead of designing it.

So what does the science actually say about making habits that stick?

=== PART 2: Solution & Story (Post separately) ===

Every habit follows a four-stage loop:

CUE → CRAVING → RESPONSE → REWARD

Miss any stage, and the habit fails.

But here's where most people go wrong:

They focus only on the RESPONSE (doing the habit).

The magic happens in the CUE and the REWARD.

The solution? The Four Laws of Habit Formation:

LAW 1: Make it OBVIOUS
Put your running shoes by your bed. Put the book on your pillow. Design your environment so the cue is impossible to miss.

LAW 2: Make it ATTRACTIVE
Only listen to your favorite podcast while exercising. Bundle things you want with things you need.

LAW 3: Make it EASY
The Two-Minute Rule: Scale any habit down to under two minutes initially.
Want to run? Commit to putting on shoes.
Want to journal? Commit to one sentence.

LAW 4: Make it SATISFYING
Track your progress visually. Mark an X on the calendar. The streak becomes its own motivation.

But here's the real secret weapon:

HABIT STACKING.

Link new habits to existing automatic behaviors.

"After I pour coffee, I will review my daily goals."

"After I brush my teeth, I will floss one section."

Which existing habit could you stack onto?

=== PART 3: Call to Action (Final part) ===

The difference between people who build lasting habits and those who don't?

It's not willpower. It's not motivation. It's not some special gene.

It's understanding the SCIENCE.

Habits that stick are:
• Obvious (impossible to miss)
• Attractive (paired with things you want)
• Easy (under two minutes to start)
• Satisfying (immediate visual reward)

And here's the rule that saves everything:

NEVER MISS TWICE.

One missed day is statistically insignificant.
Two missed days is the beginning of a new habit—NOT doing the thing.

Start ridiculously small. Build automaticity. Then scale.
