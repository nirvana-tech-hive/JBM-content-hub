## The Automation Foundation Gap

Organizations eager to automate often skip a critical step: understanding what they're automating. The result is automated chaos—systems that compound inefficiencies rather than eliminate them. Before any automation project succeeds, you must know exactly how work flows today.

Process mapping creates this foundation. It's the disciplined practice of documenting every step, decision point, and participant in a workflow. The clarity it provides transforms automation from risky experiment to reliable improvement.

---

## What Is Process Mapping?

Process mapping is the visual documentation of how work gets done. A good process map shows:
- Every step in sequence
- Who performs each step
- What tools or systems are used
- Decision points and their outcomes
- Time and resources consumed
- Handoffs between people or departments

The output is a diagram anyone can follow to understand how a process works—or should work.

---

## Why Process Mapping Before Automation

### You Can't Automate What You Don't Understand

Automating a poorly understood process produces unpredictable results. Teams discover:
- Steps they didn't know existed
- Dependencies they hadn't considered
- Exceptions that break automation
- Workarounds that bypass official processes

### Hidden Complexity Reveals Itself

Most processes are more complex than people realize. Documentation forces clarity:
- How many steps does "approving an invoice" actually require?
- How many people touch a "simple" customer request?
- How many exceptions exist to the "standard" process?

### Improvement Opportunities Emerge

Mapping often reveals non-automation improvements:
- Unnecessary steps that can be eliminated
- Duplicative work across teams
- Bottlenecks causing delays
- Quality issues at specific handoffs

## The Process Mapping Method

### Step 1: Define the Process Boundaries

**Name the Process:**
- Be specific: "Invoice Approval Process" not "Finance Process"
- Clear scope: Where does it start? Where does it end?

**Identify Trigger:**
- What event initiates the process?
- Example: "Vendor submits invoice via email"

**Identify Output:**
- What marks completion?
- Example: "Payment sent to vendor and recorded in system"

### Step 2: Identify Participants

List everyone involved:
- Process owner (accountable for outcome)
- Performers (do the work)
- Stakeholders (affected by process)
- Systems (tools used)

**Example: Invoice Approval**

| Role | Involvement |
|------|-------------|
| Vendor | Submits invoice |
| AP Clerk | Receives, codes, routes |
| Department Manager | Approves expenses |
| Controller | Reviews large amounts |
| Finance System | Records, tracks, pays |

### Step 3: Map the Steps

For each step, document:
- **What** happens
- **Who** does it
- **What tools** are used
- **How long** it typically takes
- **What inputs** are needed
- **What outputs** are produced

**Use Simple Symbols:**

```
Oval = Start/End
Rectangle = Step/Action
Diamond = Decision
Arrow = Flow direction
```

### Step 4: Identify Decision Points

Every "if this, then that" moment:
- Approval thresholds
- Exception handling
- Error conditions
- Alternative paths

**Example Decision:**
```
Diamond: Is amount over $5,000?
  YES → Route to Controller for approval
  NO → Proceed to payment
```

### Step 5: Document Exceptions

Every process has exceptions that deviate from the happy path:
- What could go wrong?
- What variations exist?
- What special cases occur?
- How are errors handled?

Exception handling often determines automation success or failure.

### Step 6: Validate with Participants

Those who do the work know the reality:
- Walk through the map with performers
- Ask: "Is this really how it works?"
- Capture unofficial steps and workarounds
- Revise until participants agree it's accurate

## Process Mapping Tools

### Simple Tools (Start Here)

**Paper and Sticky Notes**
- Best for initial mapping sessions
- Easy to rearrange
- Encourages collaboration
- Low barrier to participation

**Whiteboard**
- Good for team sessions
- Visual and collaborative
- Easy to photograph for records

### Digital Tools

**Lucidchart**
- Industry standard for process mapping
- Templates for common diagrams
- Collaboration features
- Integration with other tools

**Miro**
- Collaborative whiteboard
- Process mapping templates
- Good for remote teams
- Easy to share and comment

**Microsoft Visio**
- Professional diagramming
- Deep Microsoft integration
- Enterprise features
- Steeper learning curve

**Draw.io (Free)**
- Free, web-based
- Basic but functional
- Good for simple maps
- No account required

## Analyzing Your Process Map

### Look for Automation Opportunities

**Ideal Automation Candidates:**
- High volume (many occurrences)
- Rule-based (clear decision criteria)
- Stable (doesn't change frequently)
- Data-driven (digital inputs and outputs)
- Time-consuming (significant effort)

**Poor Automation Candidates:**
- Low volume (rare occurrences)
- Judgment-required (human discretion)
- Changing (frequent process changes)
- Physical (require human action)
- Simple (automation adds complexity)

### Identify Bottlenecks

Look for:
- Steps with longest duration
- Points where work waits
- Single points of failure
- Approval delays

### Find Redundancies

Watch for:
- Same data entered multiple times
- Multiple approvals for same decision
- Duplicate work across teams
- Unnecessary handoffs

### Spot Quality Issues

Identify:
- High error rate steps
- Rework loops
- Customer complaints at specific points
- Data quality problems

## From Map to Automation

### Prioritize by Impact

Score each automation opportunity:
1. Time saved per occurrence
2. Frequency of occurrence
3. Complexity to automate
4. Risk if automation fails
5. Dependencies on other processes

**Calculate Priority Score:**
```
(Hours saved × Frequency) / (Complexity × Risk) = Priority
```

### Design the Automated Process

Before building, design:
- What triggers the automation?
- What data inputs are needed?
- What decisions can be automated?
- What requires human oversight?
- How are exceptions handled?
- How is success measured?

### Build, Test, Deploy

1. **Build** the automation in a test environment
2. **Test** with historical data and edge cases
3. **Pilot** with a subset of real transactions
4. **Monitor** for issues and exceptions
5. **Scale** once proven reliable

## Key Takeaways

- **You can't automate what you don't understand**—mapping comes first
- **Involve the people who do the work**—they know the reality
- **Document exceptions**—they determine automation success
- **Look for bottlenecks and redundancies**—non-automation improvements
- **Prioritize by impact**—not all automation is equal

## Action Steps

1. **Choose one process** you're considering for automation
2. **Map it completely** using the six-step method
3. **Validate with participants** who do the work
4. **Identify automation opportunities** and non-automation improvements
